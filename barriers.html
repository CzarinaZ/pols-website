Cultural Values & Structural Challenges

Filipino immigrants and second-generation Filipino Americans often face both cultural and structural barriers when engaging in American politics. Cultural values passed down through family and tradition can shape how individuals view authority, conflict, and participation. Structural barriers include systems, and institutions that make political participation difficult, such as language access, work schedules, and complex registration processes. Together, these factors can limit confidence and access to civic engagement, even for those who want to be involved.

Cultural Values

Filipino words and short descriptions/connections below

Hiya is a value that encourages humility and avoiding actions that may bring shame to oneself or one’s family. While hiya can promote respect, it can also prevent people from speaking up, asking questions, or participating in political discussions out of fear of being wrong or embarrassed.

Pakikisama emphasizes getting along with others and keeping social harmony. This value can sometimes discourage open disagreement or political debate, especially within families or communities, making it harder to express political opinions or challenge harmful ideas.

Utang na loob refers to a deep sense of gratitude and obligation to those who have helped you. This value can shape political choices, such as supporting certain leaders or organizations out of loyalty rather than policy understanding. It can also discourage criticism of authority figures.
