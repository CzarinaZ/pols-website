Interviews

Q: What Makes It Hard To Participate In American Politics?

I learn about politics in school and on social media, but at home we don’t really talk about it. My parents say they don’t understand it well, and I don’t want to pressure them. Sometimes I feel like I’m learning everything on my own, and that can be confusing.
Shannel
12/4/2025
Student,
U.S. Resident,
Born in PH
In my family, we’re taught to be respectful and not argue back, especially with adults. So even when I care a lot about an issue, I don’t feel comfortable bringing it up or debating it at home.
Samantha
12/1/2025
Student,
U.S. Citizen,
Born in US
Sometimes I feel like my voice doesn’t really matter because people in my family aren’t able to vote yet. It makes me feel disconnected from the whole political process.
Anonymous
12/4/2025
Student,
U.S. Resident,
Born in PH
Before I became a citizen, I stayed away from politics because I was afraid it could affect my immigration status. I stayed quiet because I was scared anything political could affect my papers. Even after becoming a citizen, that fear didn’t go away right away. Also, the words on the ballot are very difficult. I’m always scared that I’ll misunderstand something and choose the wrong option.
Thelma
11/29/2025
Parent,
U.S. Citizen,
Born in PH
In the Philippines, politics can be scary, and sometimes dangerous. That feeling stayed with me even after coming to the US, so I avoided getting involved for a long time.
Kennedy
12/3/2025
Adult,
U.S. Resident,
Born in PH
I work very long hours, sometimes two jobs, so when it’s time to follow political news, I’m already exhausted. Politics feel important, but life gets in the way.
Laqui
12/3/2025
Parent,
U.S. Resident,
Born in PH
